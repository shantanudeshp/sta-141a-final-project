Abstract

This study explores neural activity data collected during decision-making tasks in mice, focusing on predicting trial outcomes based on spike train recordings from the visual cortex. Using data from 18 sessions across four mice, I developed an integrated analysis pipeline to understand and model the relationship between neural activity patterns and behavioral decisions. The investigation progressed through three stages: exploratory analysis of neural activity patterns, data integration across sessions, and predictive modeling. By applying dimensionality reduction techniques and feature normalization to address cross-session variability, I constructed a machine learning model achieving 72.17% accuracy on the test set. The analysis revealed that contrast difference betweenvisual stimuli and activity in motor-related brain areas were the strongest predictors of trial success. These findings illuminate the neural mechanisms underlying visual decision-making and demonstrate the feasibility of predicting behavioral outcomes from neural recordings with relatively high accuracy.

1. Introduction

Understanding how neural activity patterns translate into decisions represents one of the most fascinating challenges in neuroscience. The study by Steinmetz et al. (2019) provides a rich dataset to explore this relationship, recording neural activity in mice as they make decisions based on visual stimuli.

In this project, I analyze a subset of this data from 18 sessions across four mice (Cori, Frossman, Hence, and Lederberg). During each experimental trial, mice were presented with visual stimuli at varying contrast levels on two screens. They were required to make decisions by turning a wheel, with success depending on correctly responding to the relative contrast levels. The neural activity in their visual cortex was recorded from stimulus onset to 0.4 seconds post-onset, providing spike train data that potentially encodes the decision-making process.

The primary objective is to build a predictive model to determine trial outcomes (success or failure) using the neural activity data and stimuli information. This challenge presents several complexities:

  1. Neural data varies significantly across sessions and mice
  2. The relationship between neural activity and decisions may be non-linear and multidimensional
  3. Integration of data across sessions requires addressing systematic differences

By exploring these relationships and developing a predictive model, I aim to gain insights into how neural circuits process visual information and translate it into decisions. This analysis might reveal patterns that could enhance our understanding of brain function and potentially inform future experiments or analyses in neuroscience.

2. Exploratory Analysis

2.1 Overview of Dataset Structure

The dataset comprises 18 sessions from four mice, with each session containing several hundred trials. For each trial, we have:

  • Stimuli information: Contrast levels on left and right screens (values in {0, 0.25, 0.5, 1})
  • Feedback type: 1 for success, -1 for failure
  • Neural recordings: Spike trains from neurons in the visual cortex

My initial exploration revealed the distribution of sessions across mice and their characteristics:The number of neurons recorded varied considerably across sessions, ranging from 474 to 1769 neurons, with varying distributions across brain areas. This variability necessitates careful normalization during the data integration phase.

Success rates also varied across sessions, with an overall average of approximately 0.72, suggesting that mice generally performed well above chance. Interestingly, later sessions from the same mouse often showed improved performance, indicating possible learning effects.

2.2 Neural Activity Patterns

Examining the neural activity patterns within individual trials revealed several insights:

Temporal dynamics: The average spike rate across neurons showed distinct temporal patterns, with activity often peaking between 20-30 time bins (approximately 0.2-0.3 seconds post-stimulus). This aligns with expected decision-making timeframes in rodents.

Sparsity of activity: Only about 3.7% of neuron-time bins contained spikes, highlighting the sparse nature of neural firing. This sparsity is a common characteristic of neural coding and presents both challenges and opportunities for analysis.

Brain area differences: Different brain areas showed distinct activity patterns and firing rates. For instance, in Session 1, the dentate gyrus (DG) showed the highest average spikes per neuron (2.32), while the root region had the lowest (0.50). These differences likely reflect the specialized roles of different brain areas in visual processing and decision-making.

Average neural activity levels across brain areas, showing distinct firing patterns that may correspond to functional roles in the visual decision-making process.

2.3 Relationship Between Neural Activity and Trial Outcomes

I investigated how neural activity patterns differed between successful and failed trials. The analysis revealed that:

  1. Successful trials generally showed higher overall neural activity
  2. The difference in activity was particularly pronounced during the decision phase (time bins 10-30)
  3. Specific brain areas, particularly motor-related areas like MOs (secondary motor cortex), showed stronger differentiation between successful and failed trials

Neural activity time course for successful vs. failed trials, showing consistent differences in firing patterns that may drive behavioral outcomes.

2.4 Influence of Contrast Conditions

The contrast difference between left and right stimuli emerged as a strong predictor of both trial success and neural activity patterns:

  1. Trials with larger contrast differences showed higher success rates
  2. Neural activity in visual areas (e.g., VISp) increased with contrast differences
  3. Zero-contrast trials (when both screens showed no stimulus) had distinct activity patterns

Success rate increases with contrast difference, showing that mice perform better when the visual discrimination task is easier.

These exploratory findings provided valuable insights for feature engineering in the subsequent modeling phase, highlighting the importance of contrast conditions, temporal dynamics, and brain area-specific activity.

3. Data Integration

3.1 Challenges in Combining Data Across Sessions

Integrating data across sessions presented several challenges:

  1. Varying neuron counts: Sessions had different numbers of neurons
  2. Different brain area compositions: The distribution of neurons across brain areas varied
  3. Mouse-specific effects: Each mouse showed unique activity patterns
  4. Session-specific baseline activity: Overall activity levels differed across sessions

To address these challenges, I developed a multi-faceted integration approach that preserves important information while enabling cross-session comparison.

3.2 Feature Extraction Strategy

My feature extraction strategy incorporated several components:

3.2.1 Basic Trial Features

For each trial, I extracted fundamental characteristics:

  • Contrast levels (left and right)
  • Contrast difference (absolute difference between left and right)
  • Total and mean neural activity

3.2.2 Temporal Dynamics Features

I divided each trial into three phases and extracted features that capture temporal dynamics:

  • Early phase (time bins 0-10): Initial sensory processing
  • Decision phase (time bins 10-30): Main decision-making period
  • Late phase (time bins 30-40): Response execution

From these phases, I calculated:

  • Mean activity in each phase
  • Activity slopes between phases (early-to-decision, decision-to-late)
  • Early-to-late activity ratio
  • Peak firing time bin

3.2.3 Brain Area-Specific Features

For key brain areas associated with visual processing and decision-making (MOs, MOp, ACA, VIS, TH), I extracted:

  • Mean activity
  • Sum of activity
  • Proportion of active neurons
  • Peak activity
  • Phase-specific activity (early, decision, late phases)

3.2.4 Feature Normalization

To address session-specific variations, I applied within-session normalization:

This transformation converts each feature to a z-score within its session, making features comparable across sessions while preserving their relative values within each session.

3.3 Dimensionality Reduction

After extracting 371 normalized features, I applied Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to reduce dimensionality while preserving the most important variance in the data. Analysis showed that:

  1. The first principal component captured approximately 22% of variance
  2. 11 components were needed to explain 50% of variance
  3. 20 components captured approximately 60% of variance

PCA explained variance by component, showing a relatively gradual decline in variance captured by successive components.

The PCA transformation revealed that while some separation between successful and failed trials was visible in the first two principal components, there was considerable overlap, suggesting that the relationship between neural activity and outcomes is complex and multidimensional.

3.4 Final Integrated Dataset

The final integrated dataset combined:

  • Raw features (contrast levels, contrast difference)
  • Normalized brain area-specific features
  • Selected normalized time bin features
  • Principal components

This resulted in a feature matrix with 53 carefully selected features for each trial, balancing comprehensiveness with model tractability.

4. Predictive Modeling

4.1 Model Selection and Training Approach

I evaluated three types of models with different approaches to capturing the relationship between neural activity and trial outcomes:

  1. XGBoost- A gradient boosting framework known for handling complex non-linear relationships
  2. Random Forest- An ensemble of decision trees that provides good performance with minimal tuning
  3. Logistic Regression- A linear model that offers interpretability and efficiency

For each model, I implemented the following training approach:

  • Split data into training (70%), validation (10%), and testing (20%) sets
  • Used stratified sampling to maintain class distribution
  • Performed 5-fold cross-validation on the training set to assess model stability
  • Evaluated on validation set for model selection
  • Assessed final performance on the held-out test set

4.2 Model Performance Comparison

The models showed varying performance across different metrics. XGBoost emerged as the best-performing model, achieving 72.17% accuracy on the test set, with good performance across all metrics. The relatively small difference between validation and test performance suggests good generalization. The tree-based models (XGBoost and Random Forest) substantially outperformed Logistic Regression, indicating the presence of complex non-linear relationships in the data.

4.3 Feature Importance Analysis

Analysis of the XGBoost model revealed the most important features for predicting trial outcomes:

  1. contrast_diff(0.0463) - The absolute difference between left and right contrast
  2. norm_bin_31(0.0443) - Normalized activity in time bin 31 (late phase)
  3. norm_bin_26(0.0395) - Normalized activity in time bin 26 (decision phase)
  4. contrast_right(0.0360) - Right screen contrast level
  5. norm_MOs_sum(0.0308) - Normalized total activity in secondary motor cortex

Top features influencing the prediction model, showing the importance of contrast difference and specific time bins.

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) analysis provided deeper insights into feature contributions, confirming that contrast difference was consistently the strongest predictor, followed by neural activity in specific time bins during the decision phase.

The prominence of activity in motor-related areas (particularly MOs) among the top features suggests that motor preparation signals are strongly predictive of trial outcomes. This aligns with neuroscience research indicating that decision signals manifest in motor preparation areas before action execution.

4.4 Error Analysis

Examining the model's errors revealed several patterns:

  1. Contrast-dependent performance: The model achieved higher accuracy (>80%) for trials with large contrast differences (0.75-1.0) but struggled (65%) with small differences (0-0.25)
  2. Session-specific performance: Accuracy varied across sessions (from 60% to 89%), suggesting that some sessions contained more predictive neural patterns than others
  3. Confidence calibration: The model showed good confidence calibration, with average predicted probability of 0.74 for correct predictions versus 0.64 for incorrect predictions

Model accuracy increases with contrast difference between stimuli, reflecting the easier decision-making process for mice in these trials.

The confusion matrix revealed a tendency toward predicting success more often than failure, which may reflect the class imbalance in the training data where successful trials outnumbered failures.

5. Prediction Performance on the Test Sets

I evaluated the final XGBoost model on two held-out test sets, each containing 100 trials from different sessions.

The model demonstrated consistent performance across both test sets, suggesting robust generalization to new data. The high recall (94.5%) indicates that the model successfully identified most of the successful trials, though with some false positives (precision of 77.0%).

Model performance across both test sets shows consistent results, indicating good generalization.

The confusion matrix for the combined test sets reveals that while the model captured most successful trials (137 true positives, 8 false negatives), it had more difficulty with failed trials (14 true negatives, 41 false positives). This pattern suggests that neural signatures of success may be more consistent and identifiable than those of failure.

6. Discussion

6.1 Key Findings and Insights

This analysis of neural activity during visual decision-making tasks has revealed several important insights:

  1. Neural predictors of decisions: Neural activity, particularly in motor-related areas during specific time points in the decision phase, strongly predicts trial outcomes. This suggests that decision signals are encoded in preparation for motor actions.
  2. Task difficulty effects: The contrast difference between stimuli emerged as the strongest predictor of success, with both behavioral performance and neural activity patterns showing sensitivity to this parameter. This aligns with the expected psychophysical relationship between stimulus discriminability and performance.
  3. Temporal dynamics: The temporal progression of neural activity contains crucial information, with specific time bins (particularly in the latter part of the decision phase) providing strong predictive value. This highlights the dynamic nature of the decision-making process.
  4. Cross-session variability: Despite significant variability across sessions and mice, our normalization and integration approach successfully extracted consistent patterns that generalized to new data.

6.2 Limitations and Challenges

While the model achieved good performance, several limitations should be acknowledged:

  1. Feature explosion: The large number of extracted features (371 normalized features) required dimensionality reduction, which may have lost some subtle but relevant patterns.
  2. Binary prediction: The model predicted a binary outcome (success/failure) rather than modeling the full decision-making process or the continuous confidence in decisions.
  3. Session-specific effects: Despite normalization, some session-specific effects remained, as evidenced by the variable performance across sessions.
  4. Limited causal insight: While the model identified predictive features, it doesn't necessarily establish causal relationships between neural activity and decisions.

6.3 Future Directions

This analysis suggests several promising directions for future research:

  1. Causal interventions: Designing experiments that manipulate activity in key brain areas (e.g., MOs) to test their causal role in decision outcomes.
  2. Single-trial dynamics: Developing models that account for the full temporal evolution of neural activity within each trial, perhaps using recurrent neural networks.
  3. Transfer learning across mice: Exploring whether models trained on some mice can generalize to others, potentially revealing common neural decision mechanisms.
  4. Incorporating additional variables: Including pupil dilation, running speed, or other behavioral measures could provide a more comprehensive model of the decision process.

6.4 Conclusion

This project demonstrates that neural activity patterns can be used to predict behavioral outcomes with relatively high accuracy (72.17% on the test set, 75.5% on the held-out test sets). The success of this prediction task suggests that important aspects of the decision-making process are indeed encoded in the measured neural activity.

The integration of data across sessions and the extraction of meaningful features represent key methodological contributions that could be applied to other neural data analysis challenges. More broadly, this work contributes to our understanding of how neural circuits transform sensory information into decisions, a fundamental question in systems neuroscience.

Through the lens of this mouse visual decision-making task, we gain a glimpse into the complex neural computations that underlie even relatively simple decisions, highlighting both the challenges and the potential of decoding information from neural activity patterns.
